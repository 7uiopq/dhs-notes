Primary Data Secondary Data
Original data collected first-hand through fieldwork Data that has been already compiled by somebody else
Exemplia gratia: recordings, photographs, sketches, surveys Exempli gratia: books, websites, news, articles, maps, reports


Surveying

  • Easy to administer
    • Can be administered remotely via online services, mobile devices, mail, email, kiosk or telephone
  • Capable of collecting data from a large number of respondents
  • A broad range of data can be collected (e.g. attitudes, opinions, beliefs, values, behaviours, facts)


Sampling

  • Sampling is a 'shortcut' method for investigating a whole population (usually respondents from surveys)
  • Collects information from a select, small group of people within the large population
  • The sample data is used to draw general conclusions about the whole population without tediously investigating everyone
    • The sample size must not be too small, otherwise data collected will not be representative and reliable enough

Sampling methods:


Random Sampling Systemic Sampling Stratified Sampling
  • Everyone has a equal chance of getting picked, no biased data
  • Decide on the number of samples, obtain random numbers, use the aforementioned random numbers to locate samples
  • Fair way of choosing
  • Important to get representative sample (same characteristics as the population)
  • For example: drawing lots or using a table of random numbers
  • Collects data at regular intervals
  • Investigate changes, and easier to carry out
  • May miss out variations or changes in the total population, leading to biased data
  • Intervals can be based on distance, time, people, etc.
  • For example: interviewing people who are 2m apart or interviewing every 5th person you see
  • Total population is categorised into smaller groups to complete the sampling process
  • Smaller groups should be different and should not overlap
  • Reduces sampling errors and variability than random sampling
  • Data more manageable to work with
  • For example: dividing the total population into categories such as age, gender, nationality, occupation, education


Data Presentation


Bar Graph
  • Advantages
    • Effective in showing relationship between 2 or more variables
    • Show the frequency or quantity of the variable being measured
    • Display relative energy numbers or proportions of multiple classes of data
    • Compare two or more sets of data (this is true for comparative bar graphs)
  • Disadvantages
    • Unable to show data over time
  • Format
    • Title of graph
    • Label + values of x-axis
    • Label + values of y-axis
    • Shaded bars
Line Graph
  • Advantages
    • Shows changes in data over time
    • Summarises relationship and correlation
    • Easy to find trends and patterns
    • Easily extended to continue the line
    • Able to compare two or more sets of data (comparative line graphs)
  • Format
    • Title of graphs
    • Label + values of x-axis
    • Label + values of y-axos
Pie Chart
  • Advantages
    • Display relative proportions of multiple classes of data
    • Can show part-whole data
    • Size of the 'pie' can be made proportional to the toal quantity it represents
  • Format
    • Title of graph
    • Labels
    • Key
    • Coloured sections of pie
Tables
  • Advantages
    • Identify patterns systematically with clarity
  • Format
    • Title
    • Variable
    • Unit of measurement
    • Useful in Data Response Questions (DRQs)

Interconnected Places: The Geography of Stuff



Reasons for Varying Consumption Levels

  1. Affluence
    • Affluence is the state of having wealth that allows people to lead a comfortable lifestyle.
    • Higher income countries are more affluent than middle income countries, hence the citizens generally have more wealth.
    • When people have more wealth, they can spend on more goods and services, resulting in the increased usage of energy resources, food, water, etc.
    • For example, Singapore and USA fall under high-income levels while countries such as Indonesia and Sri Lanka fall under the middle or lower income levels. Hence, people living in affluent countries such as Singapore usually have a higher demand for goods and services.
  2. Economic Change and Industrialisation
    • Developed countries such as USA and UK started industrialisation in the 19th century, while developing countries such as China and Vietnam have started to embark on industrialisation.
    • As more manufacturing activities shift to cheaper locations in developing countries, the price of products fall with lower labour cost, enabling more people to consume goods.
    • For example, China has shifted towards a manufacturing-based economy from an agriculture-based economy since the late 1970s, surpassing USA in 2010 to become the world’s largest manufacturing country, producing 19.8% of the world’s manufactured goods.
  3. Lifestyle changes
    • Due to increasing economic development, high income countries are able to afford more resources in order to meet the increasing demand.
    • Citizens of high income countries are hence more likely to consume goods and services.


Impacts of Overconsumption of Food

  1. Economic Impact from Food Wastage
    • A 2019 study by Singapore Environmental Council (SEC) found that Singapore households throw away around S$342 million in unconsumed food annually. SEC estimated that on average, Singapore households throw away an estimated S$6.57 million worth of food per week.
    • Hence, due to wasteful consumption habits, money is wasted on food that will be thrown away when unconsumed.
  2. Environmental Impact from Food Transportation and Increasing Food Miles
    • Food miles are defined as the distance food travels from the farm where it is produced to the plate of the final consumer. Some food is produced locally and may only travel a few miles, while most supermarkets buy food from international countries.
    • Greenhouse gases are released into the atmosphere as fossil fuels are used for production, packaging and transportation of food, resulting in more greenhouses gases trapping more heat, increasing temperatures and global warming.
    • For example, in 2005, the import of fruits, nuts, and vegetables into California by airplane released more than 70 000 tons of carbon dioxide, equivalent to more than 12 000 cars on the road.
  3. Environmental Impact from Food Production
    • In order to produce enough food to meet increasing food demands, deforestation occurs as forests are cleared to make more land for agricultural purposes.
    • This may lead to further impacts such as soil erosion, loss of wildlife sites, and loss of biodiversity. Agricultural activities can also lead to the overuse of pesticides that pollute rivers.


Impacts of Overconsumption of Mass Produced Items: Fast Fashion

  1. Unsafe Working Environment (Social Impact)
    • In order to meet the riisng demands from consumers, workers may have to work in unfavourable conditions. Most unfavourable working conditions in factories are located in Less Developed Countires (LDCs) due to migrants attempting to escape extreme poverty, along with many other reasons.
    • Unsafe working environments put workers’ lives at risk as there may be poor electrical wiring, insufficient exits and little firefighting equipment in factories.
    • For example, on 24 April 2013, Rana Plaza, an eight-storey commercial building owned by the clothing industry collapsed in the capital of Bangladesh, resulting in 1 129 deaths.
  2. Low Income (Economic Impact)
    • Most workers in LDCs receive low income albeit working in industries that generate large amounts of cash annually.
    • For example, even though a labour passed in 2013 that allows employees in Bangladesh to form labour unions that increase their minimum wage from US$38/month to US$68/month, this figure remains below the average wages of textile workers in other countries.
  3. Water Pollution (Environmental Impact)
    • Clothing factories are able to cause water pollution as bleach and detergent are released into nearby water bodies, such as rivers and ponds.
    • For example, at the Jinxin Dyeing Plant in China, cotton is lowered into vats of dye and doused in indigo colour, releasing tons of wastewater.


Sustainable Consumption and Production

  1. Practice 5Rs
    • The 5Rs refer to Reduce, Reuse, Repurpose, Recycle, and Refuse.
    • Using the 5Rs, we are able to minimise the amount of waste that goes to landfills, by reducing the volume of waste by incineration and recycling, reusing and reducing the amount of waste at source.
    • For example, Singapore’s last remaining landfill is Semakau Landfill. 200 000 tonnes of solid waste, as well as all incineration ash produced in Singapore is sent to Semakau Landfill, and it is projected to run out of space by around 2035. If we practised the 5Rs, we can increase the percentage of waste recycled to 70%, thus lengthening the lifespan of Semakau Landfill.
  2. Reducing Food Miles
    • By purchasing goods produced locally instead of goods imported from other countries, we are able to reduce food miles.
    • For example, local producers met less than 10% of Singapore’s nutritional needs in 2019, while Singapore imported more than 90% of its food supply. By 2030, homegrown produce could meet 30% of Singapore’s nutritional needs, easing its reliance on imports and reducing its vulnerability to supply disruptions.
  3. Fair Trade
    • Fair trade is an international movement that aims to give better prices, improved working conditions, local sustainability and fairer terms of trade for farmers and workers in developing and less developed countries.
    • Fair trade sets out to help underprivileged producers move towards self-sufficiency and sustainability.
    • For example, Kuapa Kokoo, a co-operative of farmers located in Ghana, sells part of its cocoa bean crop to Divine Chocolate Ltd in the UK. This helps the farmers receive an extra US$150 per tonne for their cocoa, which is about 10% more than the usual price on the world market. Moreover, farmers receive training to help them deal with problems such as pests or diseases that affect the cocoa crop.
