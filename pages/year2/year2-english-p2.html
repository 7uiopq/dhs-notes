Section A



Main Message/Aim

  • Usually the biggest sentence found in the visual text, or the title
  • Summarises the main message of the visual text (read the visual text first)
  • Usually contains an action or informative fact (not something vague and obscure such as "wanted trendsetters" or "aspiring people")


Target Audience

  • Either mentioned or hinted in the poster (if the target audience is hinted, it's easy to miss it out, make sure to really dive within the visual text)
    • Some target audiences are obvious. If the visual text mentions cats and pets repeatedly, the target audience is most likely those who own or care about cats.
    • Target audiences can mostly be found within quotes, such as "and all this cruelty of sharks for a bowl of soup", which suggests that the target audience are those who consume shark fin soup.
  • Pay close attention to the language used in the visual text, such as pronouns, and infer conclusions.
    • For example, "the amusement park has received raving reviews from teens aged 13-18. YOU should visit us today!" shows that the target audience is likely to be teenagers from 13 to 18 years old.
  • Look at the sponsors and companies involved

Intended Effect

  • Answers to 'intended effect' type questions should always include an emotion that the visual text makes people feel, and an action that the visual text does
    • For example, visual texts may make people feel guilty and remorseful, which convinces people to stop/start doing...
  • Look at the language used in the specific phrase, such as pronouns used
    • For example, "they are paying for OUR mistakes with their lives" makes the reader feel guilty with themselves ('our' pronoun) instead of anger at other people for being irresponsible.
  • Action of poster should support the main message/aim of the poster.

Purpose

  • Target audience accompanied with main aim or action
  • Elaborate on main aim or action if required or suitable

Supporting Message (Image)

  • Provide a brief description of the image, as well as the main message the image is trying to convey
  • Pay attention to what stands out in the picture and describe significant parts of the picture, drawing links to the main message as you do so
    • Adding on to the shark fin soup example, an image of a shark with a missing fin was given. The detail that makes it outstanding and unexpected is the missing, chopped-off fin, hence your answer should focus on that.

More General Tips

  • When the question asks for x ways/methods/solutions/problems/et cetera, make sure to only give x answers, otherwise marks will be deducted for insufficient answers or Excess Denied (ED)
    • For example, if the question asks to "give two ways on how citizens can register their e-scooter," only give TWO answers.
  • Understand the criteria and defnition of the words
    • 'trendsetters' has the idea of new people wanting to follow, hence the meaning of the word 'trendsetter' should resolve around the concepts of 'new' and 'follow'.


Section B



Explain with three details...

  • Identify the relevant phrases (details) based on the keywords in the question.
    • Example: If the question states to "explain how the language used in the paragraph makes the creature appear dangerous. Support your answer with three details", make sure to focus on the keyword 'dangerous' and find supporting details that relate to 'dangerous'.
    • Details should be unique and details should be able to provide different explanations.
  • Interpret the effect of the phrases.
  • Phrase the answer to include the relevant phrases, the interpretation of their effect which is linked to the key words in the question.
    • The answer format should be:
      • 'Phrase number one' suggests that...
      • 'Phrase number two' suggests that...
      • 'Phrase number three' suggests that...
      • Link back to main idea

Unusual & Effective

  • Firstly, describe what is unusual about the phrase
    • Suggest the usual context in which words in the phrase are used to explain why it is unusual in the context given in the question
  • Effective is usually used to emphasise something
    • Infer how the unusual use of the phrase serves to help the reader understand the context better
    • Use magnifying words like 'extremely', 'very', 'immensely', 'tremendously'

Explain Fully

  • Include evidence from the passage as well as your inference
  • Link back to the question's requirement

Global Questions

  • Look at the main, overaching idea present in the required paragraphs specified within the global question
  • Make a title for each paragraph and ask yourself what the purpose of each paragraph is
  • Analyse the language used in the required paragraphs


Section C



Talking Heads

  • Look carefully at which person you are justifying, along with the opinions that they have stated as well as the required paragraph specified within the question
  • Closely analyse the question's requirements
    • Sometimes there are specific examples to choose from, for instance, when the question asks for economic problems while the required passage has both economic and social problems.

Summary

  • Look at the paragraphs required and the main idea
  • Identify at least 8 different points in order to achieve maximum content marks
  • Paraphrase keywords to the best of your ability, vary sentence structure and utilise conjunctions for language marks while still keeping in range of the maximum word count.
    • According to official MOE marking rubrics, the summary style descriptor for a full 7 marks for language is:
      • There is a sustained and successful attempt to rephrase the text language.
      • The summary is free from lifting except for phrases from the text which are difficult to substitute.
      • Apart from very occassional slips, the language is accurate.
  • Ensure a flow in ideas presented
  • Look at the starting help words and find the correct point to match it
  • Use the ASAP technique
    • Analyse the question
      • Read the question carefully and its demands; underilne key words and phrases
    • Select relevant information
      • Read and examine the passage closely; identify the relevant points and note these down
    • Arrange the points
      • Put notes in order and organise them according to the demands of the question
    • Paraphrase
      • Express points in your own words and write the summary in appropriate sentences
  • Do:
    • Use your own words
    • Minimise puncutation, grammar and spelling errors
    • Use transition words and conjunctions
    • Write your summary in one paragraph
  • Don't:
    • Exceed the given word limit
    • Put your opinions, ideas or interpretations into the summary
    • Use contractions
    • Write in second person ('you' pronouns)


General Tips for Question Types



Literal

  • Just lift the answer from the question, do not paraphrase unless you are modifying the answer to fit the question requirements.

Vocabulary

  • Go back in time and read books if you haven't already
  • Go through vocabulary banks

Paraphrasal

  • Answer in your own words
  • Watch out for root words (if the passage uses 'prolong', you cannot use 'long' in your answer sa that can be loosely considered as lifting
  • Paraphrase keywords

Inferential

  • Look at the context
  • Infer (which is why it's called an inferential question)
