Expository Writing



Expository writing explains a topic with a thorough analysis and relevant evidence, and is used to convey factual information. It is written to instruct or enlighten people and gives a clear conception about a topic.

Types of Expository Essays

  • Descriptive/Definition
    • Topics are defined by characteristics, traits, and examples
  • Process/Sequential
    • Outlines a series of steps needed in order to complete a task or produce something
  • Comparative/Contrast
    • Demonstrates how two or more subjects are the same and different
  • Cause/Effect
    • Describes how one step leads to a result
  • Problem/Solution
    • Presents a problem and possible solutions, backed by data and facts, not just opinion
  • Classification
    • Breaks down a broad topic into categories or groupings.


Expository Essay Format

  • Introduction
    • Beginning of essay
    • Provide brief background to the topic and grab the reader's attention
    • Include a hook
      • Inverted pyramid (broad -> narrow statement)
      • Short anecdote (simple and brief, but is interesting and relevant)
      • Contrast
      • Quote
      • Facts, trends and statistics
    • Contains
      • End of introduction
      • Briefly state 3 main ideas which will be explained in body of paper
      • Developing points should have significant differences
      • 3 developing points can be at the start of a sentence, end of a sentence, or in seperate sentences.
  • Body
    • Longest section of the essay
    • Contains 3 or more fully developed PEEL paragraphs (one paragraph to support each main idea stated in the introduction)
    • Each paragraph contains a topic sentence
      • Announces what the paragraph would be about
      • Specific and not factual statements
      • Use signpost words to give a clear flow
        • 'first and foremost', 'secondly', 'lastly'
    • At least 4 to 5 supporting sentences
      • Supporting sentences can include statistical/factual information (have to be memorised and legitimate
      • Can also be a description or observation, such as personal experiences
    • Transition words and phrases help to move the reader from one idea to the next
  • Conclusion
    • Brings closure to the essay
    • Mandatory to restate thesis statement and paraphrase key ideas mentioned in the introduction
    • May end with insights or an appeal to emotions
      • Final thoughts on the subject to give closure
      • Insights should be close and personal
      • Types of insights:
        • Proposing solutions
        • Predicting a future scenario
      • Avoid giving new evidence or elaborating on a new viewpoint

Guidelines

  • Thoroughly explain how something works by giving information about a topic.
  • Use 'one' for a pronoun, and avoid writing in first or second person.
  • Write in a formal tone with no contractions.


Hybrid Writing



Types of Essays

  • Expository
  • Descriptive
  • Personal Recount
  • Reflective

Structure

  • 1st person POV
  • Past tense, except for reflection or "now"

Conclusion

  • Lesson learnt
  • Emotions
  • Attitude
  • Development of self (how this lesson helped or can help you in the future)


Descriptive Writing



Pre-writing

  • Think aout the intended effect
  • Include those which would serve the effect

Appealing to the Senses

  • Focus on the five senses (sight, smell, sound, taste and touch)
  • Highlight details which catch the reader's attention
  • Point out something that may be special or unusual
  • Sequence the details logically (from near to far, left to right)

Using Figurative Language


  • Make comparisons between subject and something else (similes, metaphors)
  • Adjectives to evoke emotional response and to emphasise the extent of something
  • String a number of related words together to create a strong effect

Show-not-tell


  • Focus on a set of details or actions
  • Position two or more details together to reinforce a particular idea
  • Use a chain of actions
